# Research Rules

## Coverage Standard

Default to a useful comparison, not exhaustive market mapping. A competitor read is stronger when it is supported by:

- primary brand or product pages
- multiple public sources per competitor when needed
- clear evidence for each claimed difference
- like-for-like comparison logic

## Confidence Levels

Use these levels in the final report:

- `high`: strong direct evidence from primary sources or repeated consistent public signals
- `medium`: useful but limited by sparse coverage, mixed page roles, or partial matching
- `low`: thin, ambiguous, or weakly matched evidence

## Normalize Before Judging

Do not jump straight from raw notes to strategy. Normalize first:

- who the competitor is
- what offer is being compared
- what the price context is
- what claims are visible
- what proof is visible
- what obvious gap appears

Judgments built on non-normalized notes are more likely to confuse page-copy differences with real market differences.

## Direct Versus Indirect Competitors

Keep these distinct:

- direct substitutes
- premium references
- value players
- adjacent alternatives

If the comparison mixes roles, say so clearly.

## Commercial Judgment Layer

After the comparison table is built, ask:

- Which visible differences are likely to matter commercially?
- Which gaps affect conversion, trust, or category legibility?
- Which differences look meaningful versus cosmetic?

Good commercial judgments are:

- specific
- comparative
- evidence-backed
- useful for prioritization

Examples:

- `The focal brand is not obviously weaker on product claims, but it is less trust-dense above the fold, which likely hurts fast conversion against better-proven competitors.`
- `The premium competitor justifies its higher price through proof and materials language, while the value competitor competes on simplicity and price clarity.`
- `The focal offer looks crowded rather than underpowered; the main issue is legibility, not missing features.`

Bad commercial judgments are:

- `This market is very competitive.`
- `Competitors look strong.`
- `The brand should improve marketing.`

## Visible Difference Versus Real Difference

Not every visible difference is commercially meaningful. Prefer differences that affect:

- buyer understanding
- trust
- perceived value
- objection handling
- purchase confidence

Avoid over-weighting small layout or wording differences unless the user's question is specifically about UX copy or page design.

## Honest Gaps

Always say when you could not verify:

- whether SKUs are truly like-for-like
- whether prices are current or promotional
- whether visible social proof is recent
- whether a channel had enough public information to compare meaningfully
