# Research Rules

## Evidence Threshold

Default to a useful report, not exhaustive coverage. A theme is stronger when it is supported by:

- multiple independent examples
- multiple channels
- clear functional language
- visible negative rating context

## Confidence Levels

Use these levels in the final report:

- `high`: repeated across multiple credible public sources with strong product match
- `medium`: supported by some clear evidence but limited by channel count, sample size, or source quality
- `low`: sparse, ambiguous, or weakly matched evidence

## De-duplication

Treat these as likely duplicates unless proven otherwise:

- near-identical review phrasing across retailers
- identical review text copied into listicles or SEO pages
- the same anecdote repeated in aggregator posts

Count the idea once, then note cross-posting only as distribution context.

## Complaint Clustering

Cluster by the failure mode or objection, not by mood. Good clusters answer:

- what went wrong
- for whom
- in what situation

Examples:

- "clip opens unexpectedly during walks"
- "rope frays after a few weeks with strong pullers"
- "retractable mechanism jams or tangles"

Avoid clusters like:

- "bad"
- "negative reviews"
- "does not work"

## Severity Versus Frequency

Keep these separate:

- severity: how bad the consequence is
- frequency: how often the complaint appears

A rare safety failure can still deserve top billing even if it is less frequent than a comfort complaint.

## Out-Of-Scope Complaint Types

Exclude these by default unless the user asks for all friction points:

- shipping speed
- packaging damage
- seller fraud unrelated to the product
- customer service complaints with no product insight

## Honest Gaps

Always say when you could not verify:

- whether listings are the same product
- whether complaints are current
- whether a channel had meaningful public review access
