# Research Rules

## Evidence Threshold

Default to a useful report, not exhaustive coverage. A theme is stronger when it is supported by:

- multiple independent examples
- multiple channels
- clear functional language
- visible negative rating context

## Confidence Levels

Use these levels in the final report:

- `high`: repeated across multiple credible public sources with strong product match
- `medium`: supported by some clear evidence but limited by channel count, sample size, or source quality
- `low`: sparse, ambiguous, or weakly matched evidence

## De-duplication

Treat these as likely duplicates unless proven otherwise:

- near-identical review phrasing across retailers
- identical review text copied into listicles or SEO pages
- the same anecdote repeated in aggregator posts

Count the idea once, then note cross-posting only as distribution context.

## Complaint Clustering

Cluster by the failure mode or objection, not by mood. Good clusters answer:

- what went wrong
- for whom
- in what situation

Examples:

- "clip opens unexpectedly during walks"
- "rope frays after a few weeks with strong pullers"
- "retractable mechanism jams or tangles"

Avoid clusters like:

- "bad"
- "negative reviews"
- "does not work"

## Higher-Order Synthesis

After clustering individual complaints, ask whether the evidence supports one level up of summarization:

- Is there a subtype with disproportionate complaints?
- Is there a design pattern that repeatedly fails?
- Is there a usage context where complaints concentrate?

Good higher-order conclusions are specific and evidence-backed:

- `Retractable leashes are a distinct high-complaint subtype in this category.`
- `Clip reliability is a more important complaint driver than comfort or appearance.`

Weak higher-order conclusions are vague:

- `Customers are unhappy.`
- `People have mixed feelings.`

Only make a higher-order claim when it helps the user understand the complaint landscape better than a flat list of issues.

## Commercial Judgment Layer

After the higher-order synthesis, ask one more question:

- `What should an operator conclude from this?`

Good commercial judgments are:

- specific
- grounded in the collected evidence
- useful for merchandising, positioning, prioritization, or product decisions

Examples:

- `Retractable leashes look like a structurally complaint-heavy subcategory, so a generic low-trust SKU in this segment is likely to struggle on reviews.`
- `The main problem is not styling or comfort; it is failure under use, which means reliability claims and proof matter more than aesthetic positioning.`
- `Standard leashes may offer a cleaner conversion story than retractables because their complaint pattern is narrower and less severe.`

Bad commercial judgments are:

- `The business should do better.`
- `This is a dangerous market.`
- `Users dislike the product category.`

Do not confuse "commercial judgment" with unsupported strategy theater. A judgment is only valid if it can be defended with the sources in the report.

## Severity Versus Frequency

Keep these separate:

- severity: how bad the consequence is
- frequency: how often the complaint appears

A rare safety failure can still deserve top billing even if it is less frequent than a comfort complaint.

## Out-Of-Scope Complaint Types

Exclude these by default unless the user asks for all friction points:

- shipping speed
- packaging damage
- seller fraud unrelated to the product
- customer service complaints with no product insight

## Honest Gaps

Always say when you could not verify:

- whether listings are the same product
- whether complaints are current
- whether a channel had meaningful public review access
