# Report Format

Use this shape for the final output.

## 1. Product Target

- product name
- brand
- variant or size if known
- matching note explaining how listings were mapped

## 2. Coverage Summary

Provide a compact table or bullet list with:

- channel
- source count
- evidence quality
- notes

## 3. Category-Level Readout

Use this section when the target is broad enough to support a higher-order conclusion.

Good uses:

- identifying a high-complaint subtype
- identifying a high-risk design pattern
- identifying a specific usage context that concentrates complaints

Example:

- `Retractable leashes are a distinct high-complaint subtype in the dog leash category, driven by retraction failures, lock failures, and control/safety objections.`

If the evidence does not support this kind of rollup, say that plainly and skip to the next section.

## 4. Commercial Judgments

This section is required when the evidence supports a business-level conclusion. The point is not generic risk commentary. The point is to say what a commercially literate operator should infer from the fact pattern.

Good uses:

- identifying a weak subcategory
- identifying a conversion-killing objection
- identifying a design problem that will likely suppress repeat purchase or ratings
- identifying when a complaint pattern is severe enough to change merchandising or hero-SKU decisions

Preferred shape:

```md
- Judgment: Retractable leashes appear to be a structurally complaint-heavy subcategory, not just a few weak listings.
  Basis: Repeated mechanism and safety complaints across retailer reviews and community discussion.
  Why it matters: This makes reliability and control the make-or-break buying criteria, and weak generic SKUs are likely to face review drag.
```

Weak uses:

- `This seems risky.`
- `The brand should improve quality.`
- `Customers care about quality.`

Every judgment must be grounded in the evidence from the report. Do not invent market size, pricing power, or causal claims you did not investigate.

## 5. Key Problems

For each theme include:

- theme name
- why it matters
- confidence
- estimated spread across channels
- where it shows up
- `Example pages:` immediately below the issue

Under each issue, include 2-5 example pages when possible:

- page link
- channel
- short note about what the page supports

Preferred shape:

```md
### 1. Problem statement

- Why it matters:
- Confidence:
- Where it shows up:

Example pages:
- [Page title](url) - short note
- [Page title](url) - short note
```

Do not move all source links to the end of the report. The default is issue-first, with evidence directly attached.

## 6. Cross-Channel Patterns

Answer questions like:

- Which complaints show up in more than one channel?
- Which complaints are channel-specific?
- Are retailer reviews and community discussion telling the same story?

## 7. Confidence And Gaps

State:

- missing channels
- weak product matching
- low sample size
- inaccessible pages
- likely bias

## 8. Suggested Next Step

Recommend only one next step, for example:

- verify a suspected safety complaint with more retailer pages
- compare against a competitor product
- expand to customer-service and return-reason data
